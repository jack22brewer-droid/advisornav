What Are the Penalties for Late 401(k) Contributions?

Published: April 5, 2026 Reading time: 6 minutes

Quick Answer

Late 401(k) contributions can result in serious financial penalties. The Department of Labor (DOL) can assess civil penalties of up to $250 per day per late contribution. The IRS imposes an excise tax of 15% on prohibited transactions related to failed deposits. Beyond regulatory fines, delayed deposits mean employees lose valuable investment earnings on their contributions, creating potential ERISA liability and lawsuits. For a mid-sized company, even a single month of late deposits can trigger penalties exceeding $10,000, plus the cost of litigation and reputational damage.

Understanding 401(k) Contribution Deadlines

One of the most critical responsibilities for employers sponsoring 401(k) plans is ensuring that employee salary deferrals reach the plan's trust account on time. Many business owners don't realize that there are strict federal deadlines for depositing these contributions, and missing them can trigger substantial penalties from both the Department of Labor and the Internal Revenue Service.

The standard requirement is that employee deferrals must be deposited by the earliest of: (1) the 15th business day of the month following the month in which the amounts were withheld from employee paychecks, or (2) the date specified in your plan documents. For most plans, this means payroll contributions withheld in January must be deposited by February 15th.

Many HR professionals and business owners are surprised to learn how narrow this window is. In practice, this gives you roughly one month from the date wages are withheld to deposit the funds. A delay of even a few days beyond the deadline can trigger penalties.

DOL Penalties: $250 Per Day, Per Contribution

When the Department of Labor identifies a late 401(k) contribution deposit, they have significant enforcement authority. Under ERISA (the Employee Retirement Income Security Act), the DOL can assess civil penalties of up to $250 per day for each violation.

Here's what makes this particularly expensive: if you have 30 employees and miss the deadline by 10 days, that's potentially 30 contributions × 10 days × $250 = $75,000 in penalties. This calculation doesn't require a pattern of violations—a single month of late deposits can generate six-figure fines for larger companies.

The DOL considers several factors when determining actual penalty amounts:

  • The number of employees affected
  • The length of the delay
  • Whether the violation was intentional or inadvertent
  • The company's history of compliance
  • The amount of money involved

Even if a company has a previously clean record, a significant delay can result in substantial penalties. The DOL has moved aggressively in recent years to enforce these requirements, particularly after the 2020-2021 economic disruptions when some companies struggled with cash flow.

IRS Excise Tax on Prohibited Transactions

Beyond the DOL penalties, the IRS gets involved when late contributions are treated as prohibited transactions. While not every late deposit automatically triggers an excise tax, significant delays or patterns of non-compliance can result in the IRS assessing a 15% excise tax on the amounts involved.

Additionally, the IRS may assess penalties for failure to file Form 5330 (Return of Excise Taxes Related to Employee Benefit Plans) or other required filings. These penalties can range from $25 to $100 per day of non-filing, further compounding the financial damage.

The most serious scenario occurs when late contributions are recharacterized as employer contributions rather than employee deferrals. This creates additional tax complications and potential double taxation for affected employees.

Lost Investment Earnings and Employee Damages

While regulatory penalties are significant, the economic harm to employees from late deposits often exceeds the fines themselves. When contributions are delayed, employees miss out on investment growth during that period.

Consider this example: An employee with $500 in monthly deferrals delayed by 30 days in a growth-oriented portfolio averaging 8% annual returns loses approximately $3.33 in investment earnings just from that single month's delay. Over a 30-year career with consistent late deposits, this compounds to thousands of dollars in lost retirement savings per employee.

Under ERISA, employees and their representatives can sue companies for these losses. Class action lawsuits have become increasingly common, with some settlements exceeding $100 million for large employers with widespread deposit delays.

Real-World Penalty Scenarios

Scenario 1: Manufacturing Company (Sarah's Situation)

Sarah is the HR Director at a 50-person manufacturing firm. In January 2025, the company experienced temporary cash flow challenges and delayed their payroll contributions by 15 days (missing the February 15th deadline, depositing instead on March 1st).

The DOL conducted an audit during a routine compliance review:

  • 50 employees × 15 days late × $250/day = $187,500 in potential DOL penalties
  • Lost investment earnings across all accounts: approximately $18,000
  • Legal fees to address the violation: $12,000
  • Costs to implement monitoring systems and retroactive corrections: $8,000

The company negotiated a settlement of $95,000 in penalties, plus costs of correcting employee accounts and implementing oversight procedures. The reputational damage made recruiting and retaining talent more difficult.

Scenario 2: Professional Services Firm (Michael's Situation)

Michael owns a 15-person accounting firm. His payroll processor misconfigured the deposit schedule, and contributions were consistently deposited 8-10 days late for six consecutive months before anyone noticed.

An employee preparing her tax return noticed the late deposits and filed a complaint with the DOL:

  • 6 months × 15 employees × 9 days average × $250 = $202,500 in DOL exposure
  • IRS excise tax (15% on $18,000 total delayed): $2,700
  • Class action lawsuit filed by 12 employees seeking lost earnings damages: $47,000 settlement
  • Plan audit and remediation: $15,000

Total cost: approximately $267,200, plus the time and stress of litigation. The firm also faced mandatory ERISA education and increased compliance oversight for three years.

ERISA Fiduciary Liability

It's important to understand that failing to make timely 401(k) contributions is a fiduciary breach under ERISA. Company officers and board members can be held personally liable for these breaches, not just the corporation itself.

Additionally, if the company is insured with a fiduciary liability policy, such claims may not be covered if the violation was deemed to have been intentional or resulted from gross negligence. This means executives could face personal legal and financial exposure.

How to Prevent Late Contributions

Given the severity of penalties, prevention is critical. Here are essential steps:

  • Calendar reminders: Set alerts at least 5 business days before the deadline
  • Dedicated account: Maintain a separate bank account for payroll withholdings, making deposits impossible to accidentally skip
  • Payroll processor review: Verify your payroll company's deposit schedule matches your plan's requirements
  • Quarterly reconciliation: Have your plan administrator or accountant verify deposits against payroll records
  • ERISA training: Ensure HR and finance staff understand the legal requirements and consequences
  • Plan document review: Some plans allow extended deposit deadlines (Safe Harbor provisions); confirm what your plan allows

FAQ: Late 401(k) Contributions

What is the maximum daily penalty for late 401(k) contributions?

The Department of Labor can assess civil penalties of up to $250 per day for each late contribution. For a company with 50 employees and multiple late deposits, this can quickly accumulate to substantial amounts. A company with a 10-day delay across 50 employees faces potential penalties of $125,000.

When are 401(k) contributions considered late?

Contributions are considered late if they are not deposited into the 401(k) plan by the earliest of: (1) 15 business days after the end of the month in which they were withheld, or (2) according to the payroll frequency specified in your plan documents. For most companies, this creates a deposit deadline of the 15th or 20th of the following month.

What is the IRS excise tax for prohibited transactions in 401(k) plans?

The IRS imposes a 15% excise tax on prohibited transactions. If an employer fails to remit contributions within the required timeframe, it may be treated as a prohibited transaction, resulting in both the 15% excise tax and potential additional penalties. The excise tax applies to the full amount of the late contribution.

Can employees sue if their 401(k) contributions are deposited late?

Yes. Under ERISA, employees can pursue legal action against employers for fiduciary breaches. Late contributions mean lost investment earnings, and employees can claim damages for the difference between what their account would have been worth with timely deposits versus what they actually received. These claims can be brought individually or as class actions, potentially exposing the company to six or seven-figure liability.
