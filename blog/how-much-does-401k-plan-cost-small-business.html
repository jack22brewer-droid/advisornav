How Much Does It Cost to Set Up a 401(k) Plan for Your Small Business?

April 6, 2026 8 min read
Quick answer: A small business 401(k) plan typically costs $0–$3,000 to set up and $1,000–$5,000 per year in ongoing administration fees, plus 0.5%–1.5% of plan assets in investment expenses. However, SECURE Act 2.0 (in effect since 2023) provides tax credits up to $5,000/year for three years — totaling $15,000 — to offset startup costs for businesses with fewer than 100 employees. For businesses with under 50 employees, startup costs are often 100% offset by these credits, making the first three years essentially free. The real ongoing cost is employer contributions (optional), which range from 0% to 4% of eligible payroll depending on plan design.

The 4 Cost Categories of a Small Business 401(k)

Most business owners focus only on setup costs and miss the full picture. A 401(k) plan has four distinct cost buckets:

Cost Category Typical Range Who Pays
Plan setup / document fees $0 – $3,000 (one-time) Employer; often waived by large providers
Annual administration fees (recordkeeping, compliance, Form 5500 filing) $1,000 – $5,000/year + ~$15–$50/participant Employer or plan assets
Investment fees (expense ratios on mutual funds/ETFs inside the plan) 0.03% – 1.5% of assets annually Participants (deducted from returns)
Employer contributions (optional match or profit sharing) 0% – 4% of eligible payroll; safe harbor requires 3–4% Employer

The largest variable cost is employer contributions — but those are also the most flexible. You can run a 401(k) with zero employer match (though this may limit how much highly-compensated employees can defer). Note: The $19,500 safe harbor contribution is also fully tax-deductible as a business expense, reducing the owner's taxable income. At a 37% marginal rate, that's an additional $7,215 in federal tax savings — bringing the net cost well under $11,000 in Year 1, and the safe harbor unlocks the owner's ability to contribute up to $72,000 personally to their own retirement account.

Scenario 2: 1-Person Business (Solo Owner, No W-2 Employees)

For a sole proprietor or single-member LLC owner with no W-2 employees, a safe harbor 401(k) isn't needed at all — and neither is the discrimination testing complexity. The best option is typically a Solo 401(k) (also called an Individual 401(k) or i401(k)):

  • Setup cost: $0 (free at Fidelity, Vanguard, Schwab)
  • Annual admin cost: $0–$200
  • No per-participant fees
  • Contribution limit: same as full 401(k) — up to $72,000 in 2026

A solo 401(k) lets a self-employed person contribute as both "employer" (profit sharing up to 25% of compensation) and "employee" (elective deferral up to $24,500 in 2026). The plan becomes subject to 5500 reporting if assets exceed $250,000. Once you hire a W-2 employee (other than your spouse), you must upgrade to a full 401(k) plan.

The SECURE Act 2.0 Tax Credits: What Most Advisors Miss

The SECURE Act 2.0 (signed December 2022, fully in effect for plans established in 2023 or later) significantly changed the economics of new 401(k) plans for small businesses. Here are the two credits that apply:

1. Startup Cost Credit (Form 8881)

For businesses with 100 or fewer employees, the credit equals:

  • Fewer than 50 employees: 100% of eligible startup costs, capped at $5,000/year for 3 years = up to $15,000 total
  • 50–100 employees: 50% of eligible startup costs, same cap

Eligible startup costs include plan administration fees, employee education costs, and TPA setup fees. Investment costs (expense ratios) are not eligible. This credit directly offsets your federal tax bill — not just a deduction.

2. Employer Contribution Credit (Form 8881, Section 102)

Also new in SECURE 2.0: a credit for employer contributions to new plans, available for plans established after December 29, 2022, covering:

  • Years 1–2: 100% of employer contributions, up to $1,000 per employee (capped at employees earning under $100K)
  • Year 3: 75% credit
  • Year 4: 50% credit
  • Year 5: 25% credit

For a 10-person company with 8 eligible employees, this credit can amount to $8,000 in Year 1 alone — on top of the $5,000 startup credit.

For a business with 10 employees established in 2025, the combined SECURE 2.0 credits can offset $5,000 (startup) + $8,000 (employer contribution credit in Year 1) = $13,000 in the first year. Over 5 years, total credits can exceed $20,000 — often exceeding total administration costs. Many CPA firms are not alerting clients to this opportunity. If your accountant hasn't mentioned it, ask about Form 8881.

How to Actually Compare Providers

The 401(k) provider landscape breaks into three tiers for small businesses:

Provider Type Best For Typical Cost Limitations
Large brokerages (Fidelity, Vanguard, Schwab) Solo 401(k) and very small plans; cost-conscious owners $0–$500/year Limited investment customization; self-service compliance
Bundled fintech providers (Human Interest, Guideline, Betterment at Work) Businesses wanting turnkey setup with payroll integration $150–$500/mo base + $4–$8/participant/mo Less flexibility; limited fund selection; may not offer TPA services
Third-Party Administrator (TPA) + custodian Businesses needing custom plan design (safe harbor, profit sharing, defined benefit) $2,000–$5,000/year TPA fee + custodian fees More complex; requires coordination between TPA and custodian

For most small businesses (5–50 employees), a bundled fintech provider is a reasonable starting point — low friction to set up, automatic payroll integration, and SECURE 2.0 compliance built in. For businesses where the owner wants to maximize their own retirement savings through safe harbor + profit sharing, a TPA is typically worth the extra cost. The Hidden Cost: Investment Fees Inside the Plan

Most business owners focus on administration fees and overlook the ongoing cost hidden inside the plan: investment expense ratios. These fees are charged by the mutual funds or ETFs held within the 401(k) and are deducted directly from participant returns — employees never see a bill, but the drag on their accounts compounds over time.

The difference between a 0.10% expense ratio (Vanguard index fund) and 1.0% expense ratio (actively managed fund) is $900/year per $100,000 invested. Over 20 years at 7% growth, that's roughly $45,000 per participant in lost compounding. ERISA requires plan fiduciaries to ensure plan fees are reasonable — including investment fees. ERISA fiduciary liability is a real exposure for business owners who select high-fee funds and never review them.

Best practice: include at least one index fund option with an expense ratio below 0.20% in every plan investment lineup, and document your review process annually.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I set up a 401(k) plan mid-year for my small business?

Yes — a traditional 401(k) can be established at any point during the calendar year. However, a safe harbor 401(k) must be set up before the start of the plan year to be effective for that full year (with one exception: the 3% non-elective safe harbor can be adopted as late as December 1 of the current plan year under SECURE 2.0). If you're starting a new business, you have until your business's tax filing deadline (including extensions) to establish a 401(k) and claim deductions for that tax year.

What are the IRS contribution limits for a small business 401(k) in 2026?

In 2026, the 401(k) limits are: employee elective deferrals up to $24,500 ($32,500 if age 50–59 or 64+; $35,750 if age 60–63 under the new SECURE 2.0 "super catch-up"). Total annual additions from all sources (employee deferrals + employer match + profit sharing) are capped at the lesser of 100% of compensation or $72,000 ($80,000 for those 50+). For owner-heavy businesses wanting to contribute the maximum $72,000, a safe harbor + profit sharing combination is the most efficient path. See our safe harbor vs. traditional comparison for the mechanics.

Does my small business have to offer a 401(k) to all employees?

No — but eligibility must follow IRS minimum coverage rules. You can exclude employees under age 21, those with less than 1 year of service, and part-time employees working fewer than 500 hours/year (though SECURE 2.0 is phasing in rights for long-term part-time employees). However, you cannot selectively exclude employees to favor highly-compensated employees — that's what the ADP/ACP discrimination tests are designed to prevent. If you want flexibility in limiting employer contributions, consider vesting schedules rather than eligibility exclusions.

What happens if I don't deposit employee 401(k) deferrals on time?

Late deposits are one of the most common 401(k) violations — and one of the most costly. The DOL considers employee contributions to be plan assets the moment they're withheld from paychecks. For small plans (under 100 participants), contributions are due by the 7th business day following payroll. Late deposits trigger a 15% IRS excise tax and potential DOL civil penalties up to $250/day. Voluntary correction is available through the DOL's Voluntary Fiduciary Correction Program (VFCP), which waives some penalties if you self-report and make the plan whole. Our guide to 401(k) deposit penalties covers the correction process in detail.

Is a 401(k) worth it if I only have 2–3 employees?

It depends on whether the owner wants to maximize their own retirement savings. For a business with 2–3 employees and an owner earning $200,000+, the math often favors a 401(k): the ability to shelter $72,000/year ($24,500 employee deferral + $47,500 profit sharing) generates federal tax savings of $24,000+ per year at a 35% marginal rate. Even with $3,000–$5,000 in annual plan costs, the net benefit is strongly positive. If the owner earns under $100K or has no interest in maximizing retirement contributions, a SIMPLE IRA (lower cost, less admin) may be the better fit. The bottom line: for most small businesses, a 401(k) costs between $2,000–$8,000 per year in direct fees — before employer contributions.
