401(k) vs SIMPLE IRA vs SEP IRA: Which Retirement Plan Is Best for Your Small Business?

March 23, 2026 • 8 min read

The best retirement plan for your small business depends on three factors: how many employees you have, how much you want to contribute, and whether you need employees to contribute their own money. A SEP IRA works best for self-employed individuals and businesses that want large, employer-only contributions with minimal paperwork. A SIMPLE IRA suits businesses with under 100 employees that want an affordable plan with employee deferrals. A 401(k) is the most flexible option — higher contribution limits, loan provisions, Roth options, and the ability to customize vesting and matching — making it the strongest choice for businesses planning to grow.

Choosing a retirement plan is one of the most consequential financial decisions a small business owner makes. The right plan attracts and retains talent, reduces your tax burden, and builds long-term wealth for you and your team. The wrong plan costs you money in unnecessary fees, limits your savings potential, or creates administrative headaches that distract from running your business.

The three most common retirement plan options for small businesses are the 401(k), the SIMPLE IRA, and the SEP IRA. Each has distinct rules around who contributes, how much they can contribute, and what administrative work is required. This guide breaks down each plan type so you can make an informed decision — or know exactly what to ask your financial advisor.

SEP IRA: Maximum Simplicity, Employer-Funded Only

A Simplified Employee Pension (SEP) IRA is the easiest retirement plan to set up and maintain. There's no annual Form 5500 filing, no compliance testing, and no TPA required. You can establish a SEP IRA by completing a one-page IRS Form 5305-SEP and opening accounts at any major brokerage.

The defining feature of a SEP IRA is that only the employer contributes. Employees cannot make salary deferrals. In 2026, employers can contribute up to 25% of each eligible employee's compensation, with a maximum of $70,000 per person. For self-employed individuals, the calculation is slightly different — you can contribute up to 20% of net self-employment income after the self-employment tax deduction.

The catch is the uniformity requirement. Whatever percentage you contribute for yourself, you must contribute the same percentage for every eligible employee. If you contribute 15% of your own compensation, you owe 15% for each qualifying employee. This makes SEP IRAs expensive for business owners with multiple employees who want to maximize their own contributions.

A SEP IRA is ideal if you're self-employed with no full-time employees, or if you want the flexibility to make large contributions in profitable years and reduce or skip contributions in lean years. Contributions are entirely discretionary.

Who should choose a SEP IRA?

Freelancers, independent consultants, sole proprietors, and small businesses with few or no employees where the owner wants to maximize tax-deferred savings with zero administrative burden.

SIMPLE IRA: The Middle Ground for Small Teams

A Savings Incentive Match Plan for Employees (SIMPLE) IRA is designed for businesses with 100 or fewer employees. Unlike a SEP, a SIMPLE IRA allows employees to make salary deferral contributions — up to $16,500 in 2026, with an additional $3,500 catch-up contribution for employees age 50 and older.

The employer is required to make contributions through one of two formulas. The first option is a dollar-for-dollar match of employee deferrals up to 3% of compensation (this can be reduced to as low as 1% in two out of any five years). The second option is a flat 2% nonelective contribution for all eligible employees, regardless of whether they defer. There's no discretion here — you must choose one formula each year and follow through.

Administration is relatively light. There's no Form 5500 filing, no nondiscrimination testing, and no TPA needed. However, SIMPLE IRAs come with restrictions that 401(k) plans don't have. There are no Roth contributions, no plan loans, no hardship withdrawals, and the two-year rule means participants face a 25% early withdrawal penalty (instead of the standard 10%) if they take money out within the first two years of participation.

Who should choose a SIMPLE IRA?

Small businesses with 5 to 100 employees that want a plan with employee deferrals but don't want the cost and complexity of a 401(k). It's a solid starter plan for companies where the owner values simplicity over flexibility.

401(k): Maximum Flexibility and Savings Power

A 401(k) plan is the most versatile retirement plan available to small businesses. It offers the highest employee deferral limits ($23,500 in 2026, plus $7,500 catch-up for age 50+), optional employer matching or profit-sharing contributions, Roth contribution options, plan loans, and a wide range of vesting schedules.

Total contributions to a 401(k) — combining employee deferrals and employer contributions — can reach $70,000 per person in 2026 ($77,500 with catch-up). That's significantly more savings capacity than either a SEP or SIMPLE IRA for most scenarios, especially when both the employer and employee are contributing.

The trade-off is complexity and cost. A 401(k) requires a plan document, a TPA for annual compliance testing and Form 5500 filing, and typically involves recordkeeper fees. All-in costs for a small business 401(k) generally run $1,500 to $5,000 per year, plus per-participant fees. However, SECURE 2.0 tax credits can offset up to 100% of startup costs for businesses with up to 50 employees — covering up to $5,000 per year for the first three years, plus an additional credit for employer contributions.

For businesses that want to pass nondiscrimination testing without headaches, a safe harbor 401(k) provides an automatic pass by committing to either a 3% nonelective contribution or a specific matching formula (typically 100% on the first 3% deferred, plus 50% on the next 2%). Safe harbor contributions must be fully vested immediately.

Thanks to SECURE 2.0 tax credits, many small businesses with under 50 employees can now run a 401(k) plan at effectively zero net cost for the first three years. The math has fundamentally changed — don't assume a 401(k) is "too expensive" without running the numbers.

Who should choose a 401(k)?

Growing businesses that want to offer a competitive benefits package, maximize retirement savings for owners and key employees, and build a plan that scales. Also ideal for businesses that have outgrown a SIMPLE IRA or need features like Roth contributions, loans, or custom vesting.

How to Decide: Three Questions That Cut Through the Noise

If you're still unsure which plan fits your business, work through these three questions.

1. Do you have employees who should contribute their own money?

If yes, eliminate the SEP IRA. You need either a SIMPLE IRA or a 401(k) to allow employee salary deferrals. If your employees expect a "real" retirement plan — especially if you're competing for talent against larger employers — a 401(k) is almost always the stronger choice.

2. Are you trying to maximize your own retirement savings?

If you have no employees, a SEP IRA or solo 401(k) both allow high contribution levels. The solo 401(k) edges ahead because it allows employee deferrals plus employer contributions, potentially letting you save more at lower income levels. With employees in the picture, a 401(k) with profit-sharing gives you the most tools to maximize owner contributions while controlling costs.

3. Is your business growing?

If you plan to hire in the next two to three years, start with a 401(k). Transitioning from a SIMPLE IRA to a 401(k) later is doable but involves timing restrictions and employee communication challenges. Starting with a 401(k) — even a basic safe harbor plan — gives you a scalable foundation that grows with your company.

The Bottom Line

There's no universally "best" retirement plan for small businesses. A solo consultant maximizing tax deductions has different needs than a 40-person company competing for engineering talent. The key is matching your plan type to your current situation and your growth trajectory.

What we consistently see with our clients is that businesses planning to grow beyond 10 employees are better served by starting with a 401(k) from day one, even if a SIMPLE IRA seems easier in the short term. The SECURE 2.0 tax credits have made this math compelling — and the flexibility, contribution limits, and employee satisfaction a 401(k) provides pay dividends for years.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I switch from a SIMPLE IRA to a 401(k)?

Yes, but timing matters. You can only terminate a SIMPLE IRA plan effective January 1 of a calendar year, and you must notify employees at least 60 days before termination. Most businesses establish the new 401(k) to begin on the same January 1 date to avoid a coverage gap. Any SIMPLE IRA balances can be rolled into the new 401(k) after a two-year participation period.

Which plan is cheapest for a small business to set up?

SEP IRAs have the lowest setup and administration costs because there's no annual IRS filing requirement and no compliance testing. SIMPLE IRAs are slightly more expensive due to mandatory employer contributions. 401(k) plans have the highest administrative costs — typically $1,500 to $5,000 per year — but SECURE 2.0 tax credits can offset up to 100% of those costs for the first three years for businesses with 50 or fewer employees.

What are the 2026 contribution limits for each plan type?

For 2026, the employee deferral limit for 401(k) plans is $23,500 ($31,000 with catch-up for ages 50+). SIMPLE IRA employee deferrals are capped at $16,500 ($20,000 with catch-up). SEP IRAs allow employer-only contributions up to 25% of compensation or $70,000, whichever is less. Only the 401(k) and SIMPLE IRA allow employee salary deferrals.

Do I need a TPA for a SEP or SIMPLE IRA?

No. SEP and SIMPLE IRAs don't require a Third Party Administrator because they have no annual compliance testing, no Form 5500 filing requirement, and no nondiscrimination testing. A 401(k) plan typically requires a TPA to handle annual testing, plan document amendments, and government filings.

Can a self-employed person use all three plan types?

A self-employed individual can set up any of the three, but each works differently. A SEP IRA is often the best fit for solo self-employed individuals because it allows large employer contributions with minimal paperwork. A solo 401(k) offers both employee deferrals and employer contributions, maximizing total savings. A SIMPLE IRA is generally less attractive for self-employed individuals because the contribution limits are lower.
