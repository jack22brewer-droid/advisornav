5 Signs Your Company Needs a 401(k) Plan Review

March 10, 2026 5 min read

When was the last time your company conducted a formal 401(k) plan review? If you can't answer that question quickly, your plan probably needs one. A comprehensive plan review—including cost benchmarking, compliance audit, and investment option assessment—is one of the most important things an HR director can do to protect employees and the organization.

But how do you know when it's time? We've identified five clear indicators that suggest your plan is due for a thorough evaluation. If any of these apply to your company, don't wait.

Sign #1: Your Plan Is More Than 5 Years Old Without a Formal Review

The Problem

A lot changes in five years. Fund performance shifts, fees evolve, and better alternatives emerge. If your plan hasn't been formally reviewed—benchmarked for costs, evaluated for investment options, or audited for compliance—it's almost certainly not as competitive as it could be.

From a fiduciary perspective, this is risky. The Department of Labor expects plan sponsors to monitor and evaluate their plans regularly. A five-year gap without formal review suggests inadequate fiduciary oversight. If the DOL ever audits your plan or an employee lawsuit is filed, your lack of recent documentation will be a red flag.

The fix: Commission a comprehensive plan review immediately. This should include a cost benchmarking analysis, fund performance evaluation, compliance audit, and formal documentation of findings and recommendations.

Sign #2: You Can't Name Your 401(k) Advisor

The Problem

This might sound obvious, but you'd be surprised how many HR directors can't clearly identify who their plan advisor is or what services they're providing. Maybe the plan was set up years ago and the original person who managed it has left. Maybe the advisor has never had a formal relationship documented.

If you can't name your advisor, you probably haven't been in regular communication with them. This means you're likely missing updates on fee changes, performance reviews, or regulatory updates. You're also vulnerable: if something goes wrong with the plan, you won't have documentation of advisor communications or recommendations.

The fix: Identify your current advisor immediately and formalize the relationship. Schedule a meeting to discuss the plan, review fees and services, and establish regular communication cadence. If you don't have an advisor, or if the relationship is unclear, you likely need one.

Sign #3: Your Fund Expense Ratios Exceed 0.50%

The Problem

Industry benchmarks show that typical 401(k) plan funds should average between 0.20% and 0.50% in annual expenses. If your plan's average expense ratio is above 0.50%, you're paying more than market rates. This directly reduces employee retirement savings.

High expense ratios often indicate that your plan is offering outdated share classes. For example, R-class mutual funds commonly charge 0.35%+ when institutional shares of the same fund might charge 0.05%. Many older plans haven't been updated to offer lower-cost share classes.

The fix: Review your fund menu and compare each fund's expense ratio to lower-cost alternatives. Most fund families offer multiple share classes. Ask your recordkeeper or advisor to model the cost savings of shifting to lower-cost shares. This can often reduce overall plan costs by 30-50%.

Sign #4: Participation Is Below 70% or Declining

The Problem

A healthy 401(k) plan typically sees participation rates of 70% or higher among eligible employees. If your participation is lower, or if it's been declining, it suggests either that employees don't understand the plan's value or that the plan itself isn't meeting their needs.

Low participation can indicate several issues: high fees making the plan unattractive, poor fund options, limited employer match visibility, or simply weak plan education and communication. It can also signal compliance issues. Under nondiscrimination testing rules, if highly compensated employees can't defer sufficiently because of plan limitations, you could face compliance violations.

The fix: Conduct an employee survey or focus group to understand why participation is low. Common solutions include improving plan communication, clarifying the employer match, offering automatic enrollment (or increasing auto-enrollment percentages), and reviewing fund options for appeal and simplicity.

Sign #5: You Aren't Sure If Your Plan Passes Compliance Testing

The Problem

Every 401(k) plan must pass annual nondiscrimination testing (also called ADP/ACP testing). These tests ensure that highly compensated employees (HCEs) aren't able to defer significantly more than non-highly compensated employees (NHCEs). Failing these tests can result in required corrective distributions, IRS penalties, and loss of plan qualification.

If you're unsure whether your plan passes testing—or if testing has been delayed or skipped—you have a serious compliance problem. Some older plans are chronically out of compliance but operate under the radar. This creates liability if the IRS ever audits.

The fix: Ensure your recordkeeper or advisor is conducting annual testing and providing documentation. If testing consistently fails, work with your advisor to implement solutions: higher employer matching percentages for NHCEs, automatic enrollment, or plan design changes that encourage broader participation.

What a Comprehensive Review Includes

A thorough 401(k) plan review should address:

  • Cost benchmarking – comparing total plan costs (administrative, investment, and advisor fees) to industry standards
  • Fund performance analysis – evaluating investment performance and comparing funds to peer benchmarks
  • Investment option assessment – confirming fund selection is appropriate and offers lower-cost share classes
  • Compliance audit – confirming plan operates in compliance with ERISA and IRS rules
  • Fiduciary process documentation – ensuring your plan committee has documented processes and regular meeting minutes
  • Service provider evaluation – assessing whether your recordkeeper and advisor are performing well

A quality review typically takes 2-3 months and costs between $3,000 and $8,000 for a mid-sized plan (50-300 employees). While not insignificant, the potential savings—both in reduced plan costs and reduced fiduciary liability—typically exceed the cost within the first year.
