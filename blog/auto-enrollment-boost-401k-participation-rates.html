Auto-Enrollment: The Proven Strategy to Boost 401(k) Participation Rates

March 19, 2026 • 7 min read

If your company's 401(k) participation rate is sitting below 70%, you're leaving money on the table — and not just for your employees. Low participation weakens your plan's compliance testing results, limits the amount highly compensated employees can contribute, and signals that your benefits package isn't delivering the value it should. The good news? There's a single plan design change that consistently pushes participation above 90%: automatic enrollment.

Auto-enrollment isn't a new concept, but it's become one of the most powerful tools available to HR directors and plan sponsors. The SECURE 2.0 Act of 2022 made it even more relevant by requiring auto-enrollment for most new 401(k) plans established after December 29, 2022. Whether you're launching a new plan or optimizing an existing one, understanding how auto-enrollment works — and how to implement it correctly — is essential.

Why Participation Rates Matter More Than You Think

Many HR directors focus on the investment lineup or fee structure of their 401(k) plan, and those are important. But participation rate is the foundation everything else is built on. A beautifully designed plan with low-cost funds and a generous employer match means nothing if half your workforce isn't enrolled.

Low participation creates a cascade of problems. First, it triggers issues with annual nondiscrimination testing — specifically the ADP (Actual Deferral Percentage) and ACP (Actual Contribution Percentage) tests. These ERISA-mandated tests compare contributions from highly compensated employees (HCEs) against non-highly compensated employees (NHCEs). When NHCEs don't participate, HCEs get their contributions capped or refunded, which frustrates your leadership team and undermines the plan's purpose.

Second, low participation rates mean your employees aren't building retirement savings. That's not just their problem — it becomes your problem when long-tenured employees can't afford to retire, increasing your labor costs and limiting career advancement opportunities for younger staff.

Industry data shows that plans with voluntary enrollment average 60-70% participation, while plans with auto-enrollment consistently achieve 85-95% participation. That's not a marginal improvement — it's transformational.

How Auto-Enrollment Works

The concept is simple but powerful: instead of requiring employees to actively sign up for the 401(k) plan, they're automatically enrolled at a default contribution rate unless they choose to opt out. This leverages behavioral economics — specifically the principle of inertia. People tend to stick with the default option, whether that's opting in or opting out. By making enrollment the default, you flip inertia from working against retirement savings to working for it.

Basic Automatic Enrollment (ACA)

With a basic auto-enrollment arrangement, new employees are automatically enrolled at a default deferral rate (typically 3% to 6% of pay). Employees can change their rate or opt out at any time. This is the simplest form and works well for companies just getting started with automatic features.

Eligible Automatic Contribution Arrangement (EACA)

An EACA adds a key benefit: a permissible withdrawal provision. Employees who were auto-enrolled can withdraw their contributions (plus earnings, minus losses) within 30 to 90 days of their first automatic contribution if they decide they don't want to participate. This safety valve makes auto-enrollment more palatable to employees who feel uneasy about automatic payroll deductions. EACAs also provide a streamlined nondiscrimination testing option.

Qualified Automatic Contribution Arrangement (QACA)

A QACA is the most powerful option. It combines auto-enrollment with a required employer contribution (either a matching contribution or a 3% nonelective contribution) and provides a complete safe harbor from ADP/ACP nondiscrimination testing. The trade-off is a mandatory minimum default deferral schedule: at least 3% in year one, escalating by 1% per year up to at least 6% (and as high as 15% under SECURE 2.0). For companies that want to eliminate testing headaches entirely, a QACA is the gold standard.

SECURE 2.0: What Changed for Auto-Enrollment

The SECURE 2.0 Act, signed into law in December 2022, significantly expanded auto-enrollment requirements. Starting in plan years beginning after December 31, 2024, new 401(k) and 403(b) plans must include automatic enrollment with specific parameters.

The key requirements under SECURE 2.0 include a default deferral rate between 3% and 10% of compensation for the initial enrollment period, automatic escalation of at least 1% per year until the deferral rate reaches at least 10% (but no more than 15%), and employees must be allowed to opt out at any time. There are exemptions for small businesses with 10 or fewer employees, companies in business for less than three years, church plans, and governmental plans.

If your plan was established before December 29, 2022, you're grandfathered in and not subject to the mandate. But that doesn't mean you should ignore auto-enrollment — the benefits are too significant to pass up just because you're not legally required to implement it.

Setting the Right Default Contribution Rate

The default deferral rate is arguably the most important decision in auto-enrollment design. Set it too low, and employees save less than they need. Set it too high, and you'll see a wave of opt-outs that defeats the purpose.

Research from Vanguard and other recordkeepers suggests that a default rate of 6% strikes the best balance. At this level, most employees stay enrolled and benefit from a typical employer match (which often caps at 4% to 6% of pay). The opt-out rate at a 6% default is only modestly higher than at 3%, but the retirement savings impact is dramatically better.

Here's what the data shows at different default rates:

  • 3% default: Very low opt-out rate (~5%), but employees accumulate roughly half what they need for a secure retirement
  • 6% default: Slightly higher opt-out rate (~8-10%), but participants save nearly double compared to a 3% default
  • 10% default: Higher opt-out rate (~15-20%), but those who stay enrolled are on track for retirement readiness

The sweet spot for most mid-size companies is a 6% default with automatic escalation. This captures the employer match, keeps opt-out rates manageable, and puts employees on a path toward meaningful retirement savings.

Automatic Escalation: The Feature You Can't Skip

Auto-enrollment alone gets employees in the door. Automatic escalation — also called auto-increase — is what gets them to a savings rate that actually matters. With auto-escalation, each participant's deferral rate increases by a set amount (typically 1%) each year until it reaches a cap (commonly 10% to 15%).

This feature is critical because it combats the "set it and forget it" tendency. Without auto-escalation, an employee enrolled at 3% will likely stay at 3% for years, even though they could afford more as their salary grows. Auto-escalation harnesses that same inertia to gradually build their savings rate over time.

Under SECURE 2.0, plans subject to the auto-enrollment mandate must include auto-escalation. But even if your plan is grandfathered, adding auto-escalation is one of the highest-impact, lowest-cost changes you can make to your plan design.

Pro tip: Schedule auto-escalation increases to coincide with your company's annual raise cycle. When a participant's deferral rate goes up at the same time their paycheck increases, they're far less likely to notice or opt out.

Choosing the Right Default Investment (QDIA)

When employees are auto-enrolled, their contributions need to go somewhere. The default investment option — known as a Qualified Default Investment Alternative (QDIA) — is where those dollars land unless the employee makes an active investment election.

Under DOL regulations, three types of investments qualify as QDIAs: target-date funds, balanced funds, and managed accounts. Target-date funds are by far the most popular choice, used by over 85% of plans with auto-enrollment. They automatically adjust their asset allocation based on the participant's expected retirement date, becoming more conservative over time.

When selecting your QDIA, focus on three things: appropriate asset allocation for your workforce demographics, reasonable fees (target-date index funds from major providers like Vanguard, Fidelity, or Schwab typically charge 0.10% to 0.15%), and a strong track record of managing the glide path responsibly. Getting the QDIA right is a fiduciary obligation — if auto-enrolled participants end up in an unsuitable or overpriced default fund, the plan sponsor bears the liability.

Implementation: A Practical Checklist for HR Directors

Rolling out auto-enrollment requires careful planning to ensure compliance and minimize employee friction. Here's what you need to address before flipping the switch.

Plan Document and Legal Review

Work with your plan's ERISA attorney or TPA to amend the plan document. The amendment needs to specify the type of auto-enrollment arrangement (ACA, EACA, or QACA), the default deferral rate, the auto-escalation schedule, and the QDIA. This is not a step to skip or shortcut — errors in the plan document can create costly compliance issues.

Employee Communication

ERISA requires that you provide advance notice to employees at least 30 days (but not more than 90 days) before they're auto-enrolled. The notice must explain the default deferral rate, how to opt out or change their rate, the QDIA and its fees, and the permissible withdrawal period (if applicable). Clear, plain-language communication reduces confusion and opt-out rates.

Payroll System Configuration

Coordinate with your payroll provider to ensure automatic deductions start correctly. Test the system before the effective date. Payroll errors with auto-enrollment — like deducting from employees who should have been excluded, or failing to deduct from eligible employees — create operational and compliance headaches that are entirely preventable.

Ongoing Monitoring

After implementation, track your participation rate, opt-out rate, and average deferral rate monthly for the first year. These metrics tell you whether your default rate is set correctly and whether your communication strategy is working. Share results with your plan committee at least quarterly.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even well-intentioned auto-enrollment rollouts can stumble. The most common mistakes include setting the default rate too low at 3% because it feels "safe" (in reality, it locks employees into inadequate savings), forgetting to apply auto-enrollment to existing employees who never enrolled (not just new hires), neglecting the advance notice requirement and creating a compliance gap, choosing a QDIA based on the provider's recommendation without independently evaluating fees and suitability, and failing to implement auto-escalation alongside auto-enrollment.

Each of these mistakes is avoidable with proper planning and the right advisory support. An independent 401(k) advisor can help you design your auto-enrollment features for maximum impact while staying fully compliant with ERISA and SECURE 2.0 requirements.

The Bottom Line

Auto-enrollment isn't just a compliance checkbox or a nice-to-have plan feature. It's the single most effective tool available to HR directors who want to build a retirement plan that actually works — one that passes nondiscrimination testing, keeps leadership happy, and helps employees build the retirement savings they need. Combined with automatic escalation and a well-chosen QDIA, it transforms your 401(k) from a benefits line item into a genuine competitive advantage for recruiting and retention.

If your plan doesn't currently include auto-enrollment, or if your current setup uses an outdated 3% default without escalation, now is the time to revisit your plan design. The regulatory landscape has shifted, employee expectations have evolved, and the tools to make it happen are better than ever.
